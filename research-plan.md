## Module 3 — Agronomy & Viticulture

### 3.1 Variety Selection
- Indigenous varieties best suited to Husi microclimate:
  - **White:** Feteasca Alba, Feteasca Regala, Tamaioasa Romaneasca, Grasa de Cotnari
  - **Red:** Feteasca Neagra, Busuioaca de Bohotin (rare rosé/red, PDO in adjacent area)
  - **International varieties permitted in region:** Merlot, Cabernet Sauvignon, Pinot Noir, Sauvignon Blanc, Chardonnay
- Rootstock selection for local soil pH and nematode pressure.
- Planting density (typical 4,000–5,000 vines/ha in region).
- Expected yield per ha for DOC vs. table wine production.

### 3.2 Current Vineyard State Assessment
**Known (as of 2026-05-04):**
- Vines are **maintained and currently producing grapes** — no immediate replanting cost, revenue potential from first or second harvest season.
- Variety is predominantly **Muscat Ottonel** (locally called "Otonel") — a white aromatic variety. See note below.
- Age: described as "not very old" — likely 10–25 years, which is a productive window. Exact age to be confirmed on-site (check planting records or rings on cut canes).

**Still to assess on-site:**
- Trellis system in use (Guyot, VSP, pergola) and its condition.
- Row spacing and vine density (affects mechanisation options).
- Irrigation infrastructure (if any — drip irrigation ROI analysis).
- Percentage of missing/dead vines (gap filling cost).
- Soil health tests needed (pH, organic matter, nutrient profile).

> **Note on Muscat Ottonel:** This is one of the most commercially recognisable aromatic white varieties in Romania and Central/Eastern Europe. It produces wines with intense floral, rose, and orange blossom aromas. In the Husi area it qualifies for the Dealurile Husi DOC designation. Key commercial opportunities: dry aromatic white (fastest to market, strong HoReCa demand), semi-sweet (mass market), late-harvest dessert wine (premium, lower volume). It is also a viable base for sparkling wine (Charmat method). The variety is susceptible to botrytis — which can be either a threat (grey rot) or an opportunity (noble rot for sweet wines) depending on harvest timing and weather. This susceptibility must be central to the IPM plan (Module 3.3).

### 3.3 Organic & Sustainable Viticulture
- Cost delta for organic conversion (3-year transition period under EU rules).
- IPM (Integrated Pest Management) practices for downy mildew, powdery mildew, botrytis — key diseases in humid Moldavian climate.
- Cover cropping, compost use, biodynamic calendar (Demeter certification path).

### 3.4 Local Expertise Network
**Known:** Experienced local agronomists and winemakers are available in the Husi area for advice and paid consultancy.

**How to structure these relationships (research + action items):**
- **Vineyard agronomist (Year 1 priority):** Hire on a seasonal consultancy basis (~4–6 visits/year) to advise on spray schedules, pruning decisions, canopy management, and soil management specific to the local Muscat Ottonel parcels. Cost benchmark: €500–€2,000/season for a part-time consultant in Romania. Look for someone with direct Vaslui/Husi experience — local knowledge of frost pocket locations, disease pressure patterns, and soil variability is not replaceable by general agronomy.
- **Winemaker consultant (Year 1–2):** For the first 1–2 harvests, hire a flying winemaker or local oenologist to supervise fermentation decisions, SO2 management, and blending. This transfers knowledge to the owner rapidly and reduces the risk of losing the first commercial vintage. Possible sources: USAMV Iasi graduates, retired winemakers from Vinia Husi or Cotnari.
- **Tech integration role (you):** The Python platform (Module 5) can be designed so the local agronomist feeds observations into the system (mobile-friendly data entry for spray logs, vine health notes), creating a shared knowledge base that persists beyond any one consultant relationship.
- **Long-term:** as the owner gains experience, the consultancy role can evolve from directive to advisory, reducing cost. The goal is knowledge transfer, not permanent dependency.

## Module 4 — Winemaking Technology

### 4.1 Winery Setup Options

**Known (as of 2026-05-04):** Electricity, shed, and well are already in place on the parcel. This has major implications:
- **Electricity** eliminates the cost of grid connection (~€5k–€20k depending on distance) and immediately enables refrigeration, pumps, IoT sensors, and monitoring hardware.
- **Shed** is a candidate for conversion into a winery/cellar — avoids new building construction. Key question to assess on-site: floor area (minimum ~80–100 m² needed for a small winery), ceiling height (>3.5m preferred for tanks), orientation (north-facing or insulated = natural temperature stability), drainage, and structural condition.
- **Well** provides process water for cleaning and sanitation (critical in a winery — high water use), and potentially drip irrigation. Needs water quality test (microbiological + mineral) before food-use approval.

**Revised capex estimate for Option B:** With existing infrastructure, the on-site micro-winery build-out drops from ~€150k–€400k to potentially **€60k–€180k** depending on shed conversion scope and equipment spec.

- **Option A — Custom crush / co-operative:** Use existing winery capacity (e.g., Vinia Husi cooperative) to vinify grapes. Lowest capex, lowest control. Good for Year 1 while shed is being converted.
- **Option B — Micro-winery in existing shed:** Convert shed into a small winery (200–500 hl/year capacity). Full control, significantly reduced capex vs. a greenfield build. **Preferred path given infrastructure already in place.**
- **Option C — Hybrid:** Use cooperative for Year 1–2 harvest while building own cellar in parallel. Derisk by generating early revenue before committing full capex.

### 4.2 Fermentation Techniques
- **White wines:** Temperature-controlled stainless steel tanks, cold settling, indigenous yeast vs. commercial yeast.
- **Red wines:** Open-top fermenters, pump-over vs. punch-down, extended maceration for Feteasca Neagra.
- **Natural/low-intervention:** Whole-cluster pressing, spontaneous fermentation, minimal SO2 — aligns with consumer trends.
- **Sparkling:** Traditional method (Champenoise) vs. Charmat — potential for Feteasca Alba pétillant naturel.
- **Sweet wines:** Late harvest, passerillage (drying grapes), noble rot (botrytis) potential for Tamaioasa.

### 4.3 Cellar Equipment List & Cost Estimates
- Crusher-destemmer, press (pneumatic preferred), fermentation tanks, pumps, filters, bottling line.
- Oak barrel program (225L barrique, 500L tonneaux) vs. stainless/concrete eggs for premium whites.
- Temperature control systems (glycol chiller).
- Laboratory: basic analytical equipment (refractometer, pH meter, SO2 titration kit, density meter).

**Sources:**
- Della Toffola, Bucher Vaslin, Pera equipment catalogs
- Enartis, Laffort, Scott Laboratories — oenological product guides
- Wine Business Monthly capex benchmarks

## Module 5 — Tech & Software Platform (Python Engineer Angle)

### 5.1 Vineyard Monitoring Systems
- **Remote sensing / NDVI:** Use Sentinel-2 (free, 10m resolution, 5-day revisit) to track vine vigor, identify stressed zones.
  - API: Copernicus Data Space Ecosystem (CDSE) — free access
  - Python libraries: `sentinelhub`, `rasterio`, `geopandas`, `matplotlib`
- **Weather station integration:** Low-cost IoT stations (Davis Instruments, Onset HOBO) + MQTT/API ingestion.
- **Disease risk models:** Build a Mildew Risk Index from temperature + leaf wetness data (published models from INRAE France are public).

### 5.2 Fermentation Monitoring Platform
- **Sensors:** iSpindel (open-source Bluetooth hydrometer) or Tilt Hydrometer for real-time gravity/temp tracking.
- **Data pipeline:**
  - iSpindel → MQTT broker (Mosquitto) → InfluxDB time-series → Grafana dashboard
  - Or: FastAPI backend + PostgreSQL + React/Next.js frontend (custom build)
- **Alerts:** Anomaly detection on fermentation rate (stuck ferment detection), temperature excursion alerts (SMS via Twilio or email via SendGrid).
- **ML potential:** Train a small model on historical fermentation curves to predict end-of-ferment date; detect sluggish ferments early.

### 5.3 Business Intelligence & Traceability
- **Cellar management:** Open-source options — CellarTracker API, Vinosoft, or custom Django app.
- **Lot traceability:** QR code per barrel/tank linked to digital cellar book (EU winery regulation requires cellar register — Registrul Crama).
- **Sales & inventory:** Lightweight ERP — Odoo Community (free, Python-based) covers inventory, invoicing, CRM.
- **Label compliance automation:** Parse EU label requirements (allergens, nutritional info mandatory from Dec 2023) and generate label specs.

## Module 6 — Financial Modeling

### 6.1 Revenue Projections
- Yield scenario: 5 t/ha conservative × 10 ha = 50 t grapes → ~35,000 bottles @ 0.75L (assuming ~70% extraction).
- Price scenarios per bottle: bulk (0.5–1 €/L), entry retail (3–5 €/bottle), premium retail (8–15 €/bottle), direct-to-consumer (15–30 €/bottle).
- Revenue sensitivity table: variety, yield, price tier × 3 scenarios (pessimistic / base / optimistic).

### 6.2 Cost Structure

**Known (as of 2026-05-04):** Total capital envelope is **up to €200k**, deployed in phases — ~€50k in Year 1, remainder over Years 2–3. This is the single most important constraint shaping all investment decisions.

**Phased capital deployment plan:**

| Phase | Year | Available capital | Recommended allocation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 | Year 1 | €50k | Regulatory setup, vineyard assessment + immediate treatments, agronomist consultancy, tech platform MVP, working capital for first harvest (sell bulk or use cooperative) |
| Phase 2 | Year 2 | €70–100k | Shed conversion to winery, basic fermentation equipment (tanks, press, chiller), first own-label bottling run |
| Phase 3 | Year 3 | €50–80k | Bottling line or contract bottling upgrade, lab equipment, marketing/branding, IT platform expansion, AFIR grant match if applicable |

**Year 1 €50k breakdown (indicative):**
- ONVV/APIA registration, legal fees: ~€1–2k
- Soil tests, agronomist consultancy (Year 1): ~€1–3k
- Vineyard treatments + seasonal labor (first season): ~€5–10k
- Shed structural assessment + planning permit: ~€2–5k
- Tech platform MVP (hardware: weather station, iSpindel sensors; software: your time): ~€1–3k
- Working capital reserve (contingency, harvest seasonal crew): ~€10–15k
- AFIR grant application preparation (consultant fee): ~€2–5k
- **Buffer:** remaining ~€10k

**Key insight — AFIR leverage:** If the friend applies for Sub-measure 4.1 in Year 1 (winery investment grant), a successful application could co-finance up to 50% of the shed conversion + equipment in Year 2. The €100k Year 2 spend could effectively become €200k of investment if grant is secured. This changes the math significantly — research AFIR call timelines as a priority.

- **CAPEX items:** Land improvement, trellis repair, shed conversion, equipment, IT platform.
- **OPEX per year:** Treatments, packaging (bottles, corks, capsules, labels, cases), energy, certifications, marketing.
- Typical benchmark: €1.50–3.00/bottle all-in production cost for small Romanian winery.

**Labor cost note (hands-on operator):** With the owner fully on-site, vineyard labor in Year 1–3 can be largely owner-provided, dramatically reducing OPEX. A 10 ha vineyard requires approximately 1,000–1,500 person-days/year across all operations (pruning, training, spraying, harvest). Owner handles a significant share; seasonal workers cover harvest peaks (~15–20 people for 2–3 weeks). Model two scenarios: (a) owner-operated with minimal hired labor, (b) scaled model with a full-time vineyard worker added at Year 3.

### 6.3 Funding & Subsidies
- **EU GADR (formerly RDP) / AFIR Sub-measure 4.1:** Investments in agricultural holdings; up to 50% grant for winery equipment and shed conversion. **Priority to research** — can effectively double Year 2 capital.
- **National Vine and Wine Program (PNVV):** Annual budget administered by MADR/ONVV — supports restructuring, promotion, investments.
- **APIA Single Area Payment Scheme (SAPS):** ~€250/ha/year baseline direct payment for registered vineyards = ~€2,500/year recurring income from Year 1 if enrolled.
- **AFIR** (Agency for Rural Investment Financing) — project submission portal.

**Sources:**
- AFIR — [afir.info](https://www.afir.info)
- MADR — PNVV annual calls
- ec.europa.eu — CMO (Common Market Organisation) for wine

### 6.4 Break-Even Analysis
- Model three capex scenarios: (a) €200k own funds only, (b) €200k own + €100k AFIR grant, (c) conservative €120k total spend.
- Sensitivity to frost events (yield loss insurance — PAID Romania).
- Cash flow model: Year 1 — bulk grape sales or cooperative vinification covers variable costs; Year 2–3 — first own-label revenue; Year 4–5 — break-even target under base scenario.
- APIA direct payments (~€2,500/year) partially offset OPEX from Year 1, improving early cash flow.

## Module 9 — Scaling via Producer Association

The 10 ha pilot is deliberately a proof of concept. If the business demonstrates viability, there is a realistic path to scaling through association with other local producers in the Husi area — without requiring proportional capital investment.

### 9.1 Local Landscape
- Vaslui county has a large number of small family-owned vineyards (sub-5 ha plots), many underutilised or selling grapes at low bulk prices to larger buyers.
- These owners typically lack: branding, distribution access, winemaking equipment, and tech infrastructure — exactly what the pilot would build.
- Research: map the approximate total planted area in Husi commune and surrounding villages (use APIA/LPIS public data, INS agricultural census).

### 9.2 Association Models to Evaluate

| Model | Structure | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cooperative (SC Cooperativa)** | Shared legal entity, members contribute grapes, share profits | EU subsidies accessible at coop level, collective bargaining | Governance complexity, slower decisions |
| **Groupe de Producatori (Producer Group)** | Recognized under EU CMO, can access PNVV collective promotion funds | Access to EU promotion budgets | Requires MADR recognition process |
| **Contract grape sourcing** | Friend's winery buys grapes from neighbors under multi-year contracts | Full control, simpler legal structure | Capital risk stays with friend, no shared upside for partners |
| **Private label / négociant** | Buy finished bulk wine from neighbors, blend and bottle under own brand | Fast volume scaling, low agri risk | Less terroir authenticity, margin compression |

**Recommended angle to research:** The **Producer Group** model is likely the best fit — it lets partners keep their land, share the brand and distribution infrastructure, and qualifies for EU collective promotion funding (up to 50% co-financed campaigns in third countries).

### 9.3 Tech Platform as a Scaling Asset
- The monitoring platform (Module 5) becomes a key value proposition for recruitment of associate producers: "join the network and get free access to vineyard health monitoring and fermentation tracking."
- Multi-tenant architecture from day one: each producer sees only their own parcels, but aggregated data strengthens negotiating position with buyers and certifiers.
- This also enables **traceability at scale** — a single QR code per bottle can point to the specific parcel and fermentation lot data, a strong differentiator for export markets.

### 9.4 Precedents to Study
- **Cramele Recas** (Timis) — started small, now Romania's largest wine exporter; studied their growth model.
- **Crama Ceptura / Budureasca** — mid-size consolidation story in Dealu Mare.
- **French example:** Les Caves de Landiras (Bordeaux négociant collective) — relevant model for how small producers pool under a shared brand without merging entities.
- **Italian example:** Colline Joniche Tarantine IGP cooperatives in Puglia — comparable climate and small-holder fragmentation to Vaslui.

### 9.5 Scaling Financial Model
- Model a scenario where the winery processes grapes from 50 ha (5× current) by Year 5 through associate agreements.
- At 50 ha × 5 t/ha = 250 t → ~175,000 bottles/year — threshold for meaningful retail listings and export container volumes.
- Key metric to track: **cost per bottle decreases significantly** with volume (fixed winery costs spread over more units).
